## Conditional probability

Conditional probabilities give us a way of **updating** or **revising** probabilities in the light of new information. On Monday a weather forecaster might put the probability of rain on Thursday at 50%; on Tuesday, with more information, she revises it to 70%; on Wednesday, with still better information, to 60%. In statistical language, each forecast is **conditional** on the information available at the time.

<Example title="Example 42A">
We draw one card from a pack of 52. The probability that it is the King of Clubs is $1/52$. Now suppose someone draws the card for you and tells you only that it is a club. What is the probability that it is the King of Clubs now?

Obviously $1/13$ — the information has **reduced our sample space** from the 52 cards to the 13 clubs.
</Example>

This idea — shrinking the sample space to the event we know has occurred, and reassessing probabilities inside it — is captured by the following definition.

<Definition term="Conditional probability">
Let $A$ and $B$ be two events in a sample space $S$. The **conditional probability of $B$ given that $A$ has occurred**, written $\Pr(B \mid A)$, is
$$
\Pr(B \mid A) = \frac{\Pr(A \cap B)}{\Pr(A)},
$$
provided $\Pr(A) \neq 0$. It is read "the probability of $B$ given $A$", and may be thought of as a reassessment of the probability of $B$ once we know that $A$ has occurred.
</Definition>

<Example title="Example 42A (continued)">
We reconsider the card example using the definition. The event "King of Clubs" is a subset of the event "clubs", so the intersection of the two is just the event "King of Clubs". There are 13 clubs, so $\Pr(\text{clubs}) = 13/52$. Hence
$$
\Pr(\text{King of Clubs} \mid \text{clubs}) = \frac{\Pr(\text{King of Clubs} \cap \text{clubs})}{\Pr(\text{clubs})} = \frac{1/52}{13/52} = \frac{1}{13},
$$
exactly as our intuition said.
</Example>

You — a woman with a medical background — are one of 198 applicants for an MBA programme, of whom 81 will be selected. You hear, on good authority, that there were 70 women applicants, of whom 38 were selected. <Example title="Example 44B">
Suppose $A$ and $B$ are events in a sample space with $\Pr(A) = 0.6$, $\Pr(B) = 0.2$ and $\Pr(A \mid B) = 0.5$. Find

(a) $\Pr(B \mid A)$,&nbsp;&nbsp; (b) $\Pr(A \cup B)$,&nbsp;&nbsp; (c) $\Pr(\overline{A} \cap B)$,&nbsp;&nbsp; (d) $\Pr(B \mid \overline{A})$.

<Solution>
A useful first step is always to turn conditional probabilities into ordinary ("absolute") probabilities. From $\Pr(A \mid B) = \Pr(A \cap B)/\Pr(B)$,
$$
0.5 = \frac{\Pr(A \cap B)}{0.2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \Pr(A \cap B) = 0.1.
$$

(a) $\displaystyle \Pr(B \mid A) = \frac{\Pr(A \cap B)}{\Pr(A)} = \frac{0.1}{0.6} \approx 0.17.$

(b) $\Pr(A \cup B) = \Pr(A) + \Pr(B) - \Pr(A \cap B) = 0.6 + 0.2 - 0.1 = 0.7.$

(c) By Theorem 2, $\Pr(B) = \Pr(A \cap B) + \Pr(\overline{A} \cap B)$, so
$$
\Pr(\overline{A} \cap B) = \Pr(B) - \Pr(A \cap B) = 0.2 - 0.1 = 0.1.
$$

(d) $\displaystyle \Pr(B \mid \overline{A}) = \frac{\Pr(\overline{A} \cap B)}{\Pr(\overline{A})} = \frac{\Pr(\overline{A} \cap B)}{1 - \Pr(A)} = \frac{0.1}{1 - 0.6} = 0.25.$
</Solution>
</Example>

<Example title="Example 45B">
Show that $\Pr(B \mid A) + \Pr(\overline{B} \mid A) = 1$.

<Solution>
Using the definition of conditional probability and then Theorem 2,
$$
\Pr(B \mid A) + \Pr(\overline{B} \mid A) = \frac{\Pr(B \cap A)}{\Pr(A)} + \frac{\Pr(\overline{B} \cap A)}{\Pr(A)} = \frac{\Pr(B \cap A) + \Pr(\overline{B} \cap A)}{\Pr(A)} = \frac{\Pr(A)}{\Pr(A)} = 1.
$$
Conditioning on $A$ therefore produces a genuine probability distribution: the conditional probabilities of $B$ and $\overline{B}$ still sum to one.
</Solution>
</Example>

## Bayes' theorem

For any two events $A$ and $B$ there are two conditional probabilities to consider: $\Pr(B \mid A)$ and $\Pr(A \mid B)$. **Bayes' theorem** — named for the Rev. Thomas Bayes, who did pioneering work in probability in the 1700s — connects the two, and is one of the most useful tools for finding conditional probabilities.

<Theorem name="Bayes' Theorem">
If $A$ and $B$ are two events, then
$$
\Pr(A \mid B) = \frac{\Pr(B \mid A)\,\Pr(A)}{\Pr(B \mid A)\,\Pr(A) + \Pr(B \mid \overline{A})\,\Pr(\overline{A})}.
$$

**Proof.** By the definition of conditional probability, $\Pr(A \mid B) = \Pr(A \cap B)/\Pr(B)$. By Theorem 2, $\Pr(B) = \Pr(A \cap B) + \Pr(\overline{A} \cap B)$, so
$$
\Pr(A \mid B) = \frac{\Pr(A \cap B)}{\Pr(A \cap B) + \Pr(\overline{A} \cap B)}.
$$
Now rewrite each intersection with the definition of conditional probability: $\Pr(A \cap B) = \Pr(B \mid A)\,\Pr(A)$ and $\Pr(\overline{A} \cap B) = \Pr(B \mid \overline{A})\,\Pr(\overline{A})$. Substituting these gives the result.
</Theorem>

The denominator is simply $\Pr(B)$, rebuilt from the two ways $B$ can happen — alongside $A$, or alongside $\overline{A}$. That decomposition, $B = (A \cap B) \cup (\overline{A} \cap B)$, is the **law of total probability**.

<Example title="Example 49B">
A television manufacturer cannot produce all the tubes it needs, so it buys 20% of them from an outside supplier. The quality manager finds that 6% of the tubes produced in-house are defective, and that 8% of the purchased tubes are defective. A randomly selected television has a defective tube. What is the probability that the tube was produced in-house?

<Solution>
Let $D$ be the event "tube defective" and $C$ the event "produced by the company". We are given $\Pr(D \mid C) = 0.06$, $\Pr(D \mid \overline{C}) = 0.08$ and $\Pr(C) = 0.8$, so $\Pr(\overline{C}) = 0.2$. We want $\Pr(C \mid D)$. By Bayes' theorem,
$$
\Pr(C \mid D) = \frac{\Pr(D \mid C)\,\Pr(C)}{\Pr(D \mid C)\,\Pr(C) + \Pr(D \mid \overline{C})\,\Pr(\overline{C})} = \frac{0.06 \times 0.8}{0.06 \times 0.8 + 0.08 \times 0.2} = \frac{0.048}{0.064} = 0.75.
$$
</Solution>
</Example>

<Example title="Example 50B">
You feel ill in the night, stumble into the bathroom, grab one of three bottles in the dark and take a pill. One bottle contains poison; the other two contain aspirin. An hour later you feel ghastly. Your medical text says that 80% of people who take the poison show your symptoms, and that 5% of people who take aspirin show them. What is the probability that you took the poison, given that you have the symptoms?

<Solution>
Let $B$ be the event "having the symptoms" and $A$ the event "taking the poison", so $\overline{A}$ is "taking aspirin". The medical text gives $\Pr(B \mid A) = 0.8$ and $\Pr(B \mid \overline{A}) = 0.05$. Groping in the dark, $\Pr(A) = 1/3$ and $\Pr(\overline{A}) = 2/3$. By Bayes' theorem,
$$
\Pr(A \mid B) = \frac{\Pr(B \mid A)\,\Pr(A)}{\Pr(B \mid A)\,\Pr(A) + \Pr(B \mid \overline{A})\,\Pr(\overline{A})} = \frac{0.8 \times \tfrac{1}{3}}{0.8 \times \tfrac{1}{3} + 0.05 \times \tfrac{2}{3}} \approx 0.89.
$$
We recommend that you call the doctor!
</Solution>
</Example>

## Independent events

Intuitively, two events are **independent** if they have nothing to do with each other — the occurrence of one tells us nothing about the other. If event $A$ has no bearing on event $B$, then knowing that $A$ has occurred should leave the probability of $B$ unchanged:
$$
\Pr(B \mid A) = \Pr(B).
$$
Substituting the definition of conditional probability, $\Pr(B \mid A) = \Pr(A \cap B)/\Pr(A)$, this becomes $\Pr(A \cap B)/\Pr(A) = \Pr(B)$, or $\Pr(A \cap B) = \Pr(A)\,\Pr(B)$. This gives a clean, symmetric definition that does not single out one event as the "condition".

<Definition term="Independent events">
Events $A$ and $B$ are **independent** if
$$
\Pr(A \cap B) = \Pr(A) \times \Pr(B).
$$
More generally, events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ are independent if
$$
\Pr(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = \Pr(A_1) \times \Pr(A_2) \times \cdots \times \Pr(A_n).
$$
</Definition>

Many students confuse **independent** events with **mutually exclusive** events. It helps to remember that "mutually exclusive" is a concept from *set theory* — it can be drawn on a Venn diagram — whereas "independence" is a concept from *probability theory* and **cannot** be shown on a Venn diagram.

In fact, two events with positive probability that are mutually exclusive can **never** be independent. The events "the gold price rises today" and "it rains in Cape Town this afternoon" are genuinely independent — they have nothing to do with each other — and their intersection is not empty, so they are not mutually exclusive. By contrast, "heads" and "tails" on a single coin toss *are* mutually exclusive: tell me the coin came up heads and my probability of tails drops instantly from 0.5 to zero. That is the strongest possible dependence — so mutually exclusive events are certainly not independent.
